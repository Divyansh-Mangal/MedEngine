i4KIDS India

A paediatric innovation bridge — bringing the i4KIDS model to India in coalition with The Child Lens, Babylon Children's Hospital, and Europe's children's-health capital.

Prepared for the founding coalition • Version 1.0 • Date 1 July 2026
Structure Adopts the generalised MedEngine four-entity architecture, re-scoped for paediatrics
Status Founding document — vision, coalition, structure, operations, regulatory & financials

Executive SummaryThe one-page version

The proposition. Establish i4KIDS India — the Indian expression of Europe's leading paediatric innovation hub — as a federated entity that channels children's-health innovations from European startups and hospitals into India for real-world validation, cost-reduction and scale, while surfacing Indian innovation and evidence back to Europe. It is anchored clinically on Babylon Children's Hospital, Jaipur (a paediatric specialty hospital) under Dr Dhananjay Mangal, and, once online, on Jaipur Medical College & Hospital (a large general teaching hospital) — giving the venture both a deep speciality partner and a broad study partner.

The venture is built on a documented gap: although children are roughly a quarter of the world's population, only about 1.6% of healthcare venture capital reaches paediatric projects.6 Europe has assembled the antidote — i4KIDS at Sant Joan de Déu, a dedicated €55M paediatric fund (Montana), a biomedical venture builder (Chasing Science), and specialist advisory (The Child Lens). India offers what that machine most needs to prove and scale: patient volume, a low-cost engineering and manufacturing base, a fast-maturing regulatory pathway, and demand-side financing through Ayushman Bharat. i4KIDS India is the connective tissue between the two.

What this document commits to

A legally-grounded four-entity federation (non-profit Foundation, Services company, Capital bridge, and Labs) modelled on the MedEngine architecture; a named coalition with defined roles; a child-first governance and safety regime; an India-specific regulatory and data-protection plan; and a phased, solvency-first financial roadmap that does not depend on any single funder.

Everyone named in this report as a direct participant — the European hub, the capital partners, the Indian clinical anchors, and the founding coordinator — is mapped in the active-members directory (Part 2). The European Children's Hospitals Organisation (ECHO) participates indirectly, through i4KIDS, as the design intends.

Part 1Rationale & Mandate

1.1 The paediatric innovation gap

Children are not small adults, and the market has treated that truth as a reason to look away. Devices and drugs designed for adults are routinely used off-label in children because the paediatric-specific version was never built — the trials are harder, the cohorts smaller, the liability higher, and the commercial returns thinner. The result is a structural under-investment that i4KIDS in Barcelona was created to correct, and that it now quantifies plainly: only around 1.6% of healthcare VC flows to paediatrics despite children being ~25% of humanity.6

This is precisely the failure the MedEngine white paper describes as a body that "cannot feel itself" — the signal (a clinical need) exists, but the nerve that would carry it to the people who could act (engineers, founders, capital) is severed. Paediatrics is the most severed nerve of all. i4KIDS India exists to reconnect it, on Indian ground.

1.2 Why India, why now

India is the highest-leverage second home for this model for four compounding reasons:

The Indian advantage

Volume & diversity. Paediatric patient numbers and disease diversity generate the evidence base that small European cohorts cannot. Cost-down engineering. India's device and software talent can re-engineer a European prototype to a fraction of its cost — the classic reverse-innovation loop. A maturing regulatory pathway. CDSCO's Medical Device Rules and the risk-based classification regime now give a defined route to market. Demand-side scale. Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY creates a payer able to absorb validated paediatric innovation at population scale.

1.3 Adopting the MedEngine spine

i4KIDS India does not invent a new architecture; it inherits the generalised MedEngine entity structure (specified in full in the MedEngine White Paper) and re-scopes it for a single population — children and mothers. From that structure it takes four load-bearing commitments:

  1. Connective tissue, not a brain. The entity routes, matches and de-risks; it does not centrally plan who innovates. It is a nervous system for paediatric innovation, not its command centre.
  2. A federated legal form — a mission-locked non-profit paired with commercial and capital arms — so that purpose and solvency are structurally separated and neither captures the other.
  3. Positive-sum dealings. Every role (clinician, founder, student, investor, hospital, and above all the child and caregiver) must be able to name what it gains.
  4. India-first, export-ready. Built to Indian regulation and cost, designed to interoperate with European standards so innovation can move both ways.

Part 2The Coalition

Who is actually in this. Below, each partner organisation and its active members — verified from public sources — followed by a single consolidated directory.

2.1 The European engine — i4KIDS, Sant Joan de Déu, and ECHO

i4KIDS is a Pediatric Innovation Hub coordinated by Sant Joan de Déu (SJD) Barcelona Children's Hospital and the SJD Research Institute (IRSJD). It is recognised as a European benchmark for paediatric innovation, bringing together 110+ research groups and 30+ organisations, and has had its recognition renewed for 2026–2028.1 Its instruments include a six-month Acceleration Programme (3rd edition in 2026, culminating in a Barcelona Demo Day in November 2026), the Impact4kids transatlantic accelerator run with Stanford Biodesign (Impact1), and a Paediatric Investment Forum convened with Montana and Med Angels del CoMB.1,2

Active members — i4KIDS / SJD

Arnau Valls Esteve — Director of i4KIDS and Coordinator of i4KIDS-EUROPE; Head of Innovation at SJD Barcelona Children's Hospital; investigator in the Healthcare Technology Innovation group at IRSJD. The primary institutional counterpart for i4KIDS India.3

ECHO — the European Children's Hospitals Organisation — is a collaborator of i4KIDS and the network through which i4KIDS-EUROPE reaches peer children's hospitals (e.g. Children's University Hospital Latvia, HUS Helsinki, Mary Elizabeth's Hospital Copenhagen).4 Per the coalition's design, ECHO is involved indirectly — its members are reached through i4KIDS rather than contracted directly by i4KIDS India.

2.2 Capital & venture-building

The financing side of the coalition is unusually complete, spanning a dedicated fund, a venture builder, and specialist advisory — and it is tied together by overlapping people.

Montana Children's Health Fund

The first European venture-capital fund exclusively focused on children's health, managed by Ship2B Ventures, with ~€55M and backing from the European Investment Fund (EIF) and Spain's CDTI Innvierte.5 A paediatric-first thesis: children at the centre of every investment decision.
Active members: Marc Ramis Castelltort — Founder & Managing Partner (DPhil Biochemistry, Oxford; HBS PLD); Maite Fibla — General Partner and Investment Committee member (co-founder of Ship2B).5 (Note: the founding brief referred to "Marc Remmis"; the correct name is Marc Ramis Castelltort.)

Chasing Science

An early-stage biomedical venture builder operating between London and Barcelona, which launches companies from promising academic data toward first-in-human trials.7
Active member: Marc Ramis Castelltort — Co-founder & Partner (also of Montana), linking venture-building capacity directly to the fund.

The Child Lens

The advisory vehicle founded by Pilar Puig-Sàrries, a Barcelona-based scientist-investor whose roles span Montana, Chasing Science and DIVE Medical; she also co-founded the BioExpert Network and holds an MSc in Molecular Biotechnology (Universitat de Barcelona).8,9 The Child Lens is the coalition's paediatric-diligence and portfolio lens, and Pilar is the connective figure across fund, builder and advisory.

DIVE Medical — a University of Zaragoza spin-off whose eye-tracking + AI system tests visual function in non-cooperative or preverbal children10 — is a template portfolio company: exactly the kind of paediatric-specific innovation the bridge is built to move into Indian validation.

Established July 2007, a 50-bed paediatric and maternity specialty hospital (NICU, PICU, general wards) at Adarsh Nagar, near Raja Park, Jaipur.11

A large general teaching hospital in Jaipur.13 The coalition will bring JMCH in once it comes fully online, giving i4KIDS India a broad, high-volume study hospital to complement Babylon's paediatric depth — the deliberate "specialty + general" pairing.

Founding coordinator

Part 3Entity Structure — The Federation

i4KIDS India inherits the MedEngine four-entity federation: a mission-locked non-profit at the centre, with commercial, capital and technical arms around it. Purpose and profit are legally separated so neither captures the other.

3.1 i4KIDS India Foundation — Section 8 company

The mission-holder. A not-for-profit Section 8 company (Companies Act, 2013) that owns the brand, the mandate, the Problem Bank (registry of validated paediatric needs), the ethics and child-safety regime, and the licence to the i4KIDS name in India. It cannot distribute profits; its board carries the mission-lock. All clinical-need origination and patient-facing governance live here.

3.2 i4KIDS India Services — Private Limited

The operating engine. A Private Limited company that runs the acceleration programme, matching and brokerage, clinical-validation services, regulatory-navigation support, and paid corporate/hospital memberships. This is where earned revenue accrues and where staff are employed. It contracts with the Foundation for use of the Problem Bank under a mission-aligned licence.

3.3 The Capital bridge

In the near term, i4KIDS India does not raise its own fund. It operates a capital-facilitation bridge: a structured syndication desk that channels vetted Indian and cross-border paediatric ventures to Montana, The Child Lens, Chasing Science and Indian co-investors, earning through advisory and success-fee arrangements rather than carry. As deal-flow proves out (Phase 2–3), the coalition may register a dedicated SEBI-registered AIF/VCF in India to hold direct positions — a decision deferred until volume justifies the regulatory overhead.

3.4 i4KIDS India Labs & Biodesign

The technical commons. Shared prototyping and testing capacity, a paediatric biodesign fellowship (the SJD/Stanford Biodesign method localised), and the data and regulatory tooling (evidence capture, CDSCO documentation, DPDP-compliant data pipelines) that every portfolio company reuses. Housed with, and drawing clinical immersion from, Babylon and later JMCH.

Why federate rather than run one company

A single entity trying to hold a children's-health mission and chase returns will, under pressure, sacrifice the mission — the pattern the MedEngine governance section calls capture. Separating the mission-locked Foundation from the revenue-taking Services and the capital bridge means the child-safety and needs-registry functions can never be quietly repriced to serve an investor. It is the structure doing the protecting, not goodwill.

Part 6The Bridge

6.1 Europe ↔ India flow

The core mechanism is a two-way pipe. European paediatric innovations — i4KIDS accelerator alumni, Montana/Chasing Science portfolio, spin-offs like DIVE Medical — enter India for clinical validation, cost-optimised re-engineering and market access. In return, Indian evidence (large, diverse cohorts), Indian-originated innovation, and reverse-innovation cost-downs flow back to strengthen the European portfolio and satisfy EU regulators.

6.2 The two-hospital model

The deliberate pairing is the operational heart of the venture. Babylon Children's Hospital provides paediatric depth — a focused specialty environment, NICU/PICU, and a lead clinician (Dr Mangal) whose network recruits the wider clinician base. JMCH, once online, provides breadth — a large general teaching hospital delivering volume, diversity, and trainee throughput. Depth validates that an innovation works in the hardest paediatric cases; breadth proves it scales. Together they give i4KIDS India both a proving ground and a scaling ground on the same city footprint.

Part 7Governance & Child Safety

A venture operating on children carries a heightened duty. Governance here is not boilerplate; it is the licence to operate.

Mission-lock. The Foundation's constitution fixes the paediatric-welfare objective and bars its erosion; board composition guarantees clinical and patient-advocate voices independent of the capital arm.

Child safeguarding. A standing safeguarding policy governs every interaction involving a minor: enhanced consent-and-assent protocols, data minimisation, mandatory background checks for anyone with patient contact, and an independent reporting channel.

Ethics fast-track with teeth. A paediatric ethics committee reviews every validation project; "fast-track" refers to responsiveness, never to reduced scrutiny for vulnerable subjects.

Capture-resistance. Following the MedEngine four-risk frame — funder capture, clinician conflict-of-interest, data misuse, and mission drift — each has a named control: the capital bridge cannot set Problem-Bank priorities; clinician equity is disclosed and capped; data licences are purpose-bound; and mission-drift triggers a board supermajority review.

Data & children

Under India's DPDP framework, processing children's personal data requires verifiable parental consent and prohibits tracking or targeted advertising directed at minors. i4KIDS India treats this as a floor, not a ceiling: paediatric data pipelines are purpose-bound, minimised, and audited, with EU-interoperable handling so evidence can serve both regulators.

Part 9Financials & Roadmap

9.1 Cost & revenue architecture

The model is solvency-first: no phase depends on a single funder. Early costs are covered by a blend of philanthropic/partner grants (SJD/i4KIDS linkage, Indian CSR, EU children's-health grants) and hospital in-kind contribution; earned revenue then takes over.

9.2 Phased roadmap

Phase 0 (M0–M6): incorporate Foundation + Services; sign MoUs with i4KIDS/SJD, Babylon, and the capital partners; stand up the Babylon pilot site and ethics committee. Phase 1 (M6–M18): run the first cohort of 4–6 paediatric ventures; execute 2–3 EU↔India validation pilots (a DIVE-Medical-type device is an ideal first case). Phase 2 (Y2–Y3): activate JMCH as the general study site; formalise the capital bridge; demonstrate reverse-innovation back to Europe. Phase 3 (Y3+): assess a dedicated India AIF; expand beyond Jaipur; reach operational self-sufficiency on earned revenue.
